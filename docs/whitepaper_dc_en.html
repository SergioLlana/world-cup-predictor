White paper · wcpred

A Dixon-Coles tuned for expected points at the 2026 World Cup

Model formulation, validation protocol and known limitations

wcpred project June 2026 model v. 2026-06-12
Abstract

wcpred predicts 2026 World Cup scorelines by picking, for each match, not the most likely result but the one that maximises expected points under the Penka pool rules. The core is a Dixon-Coles model — a weighted Poisson with per-team attack and defence, time decay and the rho correction — trained on ~11,000 international matches since 2015. At prediction time the 1X2 probabilities come 100% from the betting market, and the model only shapes the distribution of scorelines within each outcome. Validation replays the live protocol — a per-matchday re-fit — over six tournaments (290 matches), where the default configuration scores 594 Penka points (2.05/match). The main limitation is structural: weakly connected confederations are poorly anchored to the global scale.

§1The problem: optimise points, not correct calls

The goal is not to forecast well in the abstract but to score in a specific pool. Penka pays at three tiers — exact score, correct goal difference (any correctly called draw counts), and the winner — with stakes that grow by stage:

The key consequence: the optimal pick is not the most likely score. A 2-1 also collects the middle tier from 1-0 and 3-2, because they share its goal difference; a 1-0 only from 2-1 and 3-2 among the plausible scores. The optimiser sweeps the full scoreline probability matrix P[home_goals, away_goals] (a 0..8 grid) and picks the cell with the highest expected value for the scoring mode and the match's stage. Everything else — model, odds, simulation — exists to estimate that matrix well.

§2The Dixon-Coles model

The core is the classic Dixon and Coles (1997) model: each team i has an attack parameter αi and a defence parameter δi, and each team's goals in a match follow Poissons with rates

λhome = exp(μ + αi − δj + γ·𝟙home) ,   λaway = exp(μ + αj − δi)

with a rho correction that adjusts the low-score cells (0-0, 1-0, 0-1, 1-1), where Poisson independence systematically fails. The home advantage γ applies only to the three hosts (USA, Mexico, Canada) when they play in their own country. Because attack and defence are fit jointly, thrashing a weak side is worth less than beating a strong one: the model discounts strength of schedule — with the structural caveat of section 6.

§3Data sources and the market blend

Results

The martj42/international_results dataset (updated daily) provides every international since 1872 plus the 2026 World Cup calendar. It is the only training source: the model never trains on odds.

Market odds (prediction, not training)

For matches with available 1X2 odds (the median across bookmakers via The Odds API), the bookmaker margin is stripped and the clean probabilities are turned into a market-implied scoreline matrix, recalibrating the Poisson rates to match the observed 1X2. With the default ODDS_WEIGHT = 1.0, the 1X2 probabilities come 100% from the market; the model only decides how the scorelines are distributed within each outcome (home win, draw or away win), which is exactly what the odds do not tell you. In short: the market sets who wins and the model, with what scoreline.

Every odds download also saves a time-stamped snapshot (data/input/odds/odds_<date>.csv). When regenerating a past day, the pipeline uses the snapshot in force back then, so later market moves never leak into a reconstructed prediction.

xG: evaluated and dropped for 2026

There is a mode that blends FotMob xG into the training targets, controlled by the XG_ALPHA parameter: for example, with a weight of 0.6 on goals the blend is 0.6·goals + 0.4·xG. An experiment showed a clear trade-off: the more weight xG gets, the better the model calibrates (log-loss drops from 2.7702 to 2.7678) but the worse it scores (from 295.5 to 290.0 Superbru points). On top of that, FotMob only covers from ~mid-2022, with no friendlies and only ~28% of qualifiers.

§4From match to tournament: Monte Carlo

Two simulation layers sit on top of the per-match scoreline matrices:

  • Group stage — a Monte Carlo of the groups. Matches already played enter with their real result; pending ones are sampled from their matrices. Tie-breaks by points, goal difference and goals scored; whatever remains is broken at random.
  • Full tournament — a joint simulation of the 12 groups, allocation of the 8 best third-placed teams, the official Round-of-32 bracket through the final, and resolution of knockout ties via extra time (at 1/3 of the scoring rate) and penalties. It uses real results where they already exist — in the knockouts too — so it also works mid-tournament.

The "1/3 of the scoring rate" factor is not estimated from data: it is pure time proportion. Extra time lasts 30 minutes against the 90 of regulation (30/90 = 1/3) and, assuming goals arrive at a constant per-minute rate, each team's expected goals over that half hour are a third of those in a full match. If the tie persists after extra time, it is resolved as a one-goal win split 50% to each side (penalties).

The per-match predictions, by contrast, score the 90-minute result — which is what Penka and Superbru pay — so extra time and penalties are off by default.

§5Validation: backtest with a rolling re-fit

There is no test suite: the model's regression check is the backtest. It covers six tournaments — World Cup 2018, Euro 2021, Copa América 2021, World Cup 2022, Euro 2024 and Copa América 2024 — and by default replays the live protocol exactly: a rolling re-fit per matchday, where the model is retrained before each batch of matches using only what was played up to then (alternatively it can be compared against a single pre-tournament fit). Beyond points, each run reports two low-variance metrics: the ranked probability score (RPS) over the 1X2 and the exact-score log-loss. Methodological discipline matters here: tuning is done on RPS and log-loss, with points only as a tie-breaker — over ~64 matches per tournament, points are too noisy to drive decisions on their own. The hardest tournament is World Cup 2022, full of upsets: 1.53 Penka pts/match and 53% of outcomes correct, in line with the bookmaker favourites themselves (~55% there).

Several experiments left two lessons of opposite sign. FRIENDLY_WEIGHT 0.5 → 1.0 improved RPS monotonically across the grid and won on all three metrics under the rolling re-fit (295.5 vs 289.0 Superbru points; RPS 0.1890 vs 0.1897; log-loss 2.770 vs 2.781): it is now the default. Capping blowouts (GD_CAP) did the opposite — it worsened everything in every combination — confirming that lopsided margins carry real signal that Dixon-Coles already discounts adequately.

§6Main limitation: cross-confederation anchoring

Anchoring a confederation means fixing its level relative to the others, so teams from different blocs are comparable on a single scale. Dixon-Coles can only do this through the "bridge" matches between confederations: the few games pitting, say, Europe against South America. When those crossings are scarce, the relative scale of two confederations is poorly determined: a handful of results can shift whole blocs, and the weak-opponent discount falls short. The connectivity matrix quantifies it:

Case 1: Australia above the United States

The model ranks Australia (24th, overall rating 1.98) ahead of the USA (43rd, 1.66) even though the USA is a host. Since June 2024, Australia built a +19 goal difference essentially against AFC opposition (mean opponent 1.12; including Bangladesh at −1.3), while the USA played — and often lost to — Brazil, Portugal, Germany, Belgium and Colombia (mean opponent 1.66). Dixon-Coles' schedule discount exists, but it is only as good as the connectivity: with the AFC almost isolated (70% intra), its own "weak" sides are over-rated in absolute terms and the discount falls short. The analysis verdict: half real, half artefact — the USA really has lost 42% of its matches, but Australia's record is schedule-inflated. In practice the effect is dampened. With home advantage, the group simulation already leaves the two near-level (qualification probability 0.498 vs 0.494). And where the artefact bites hardest, the odds correct it: without the market, Australia qualified with probability 0.49 ahead of Paraguay (0.46); with odds, Australia drops to 0.26 and Paraguay rises to 0.42.

Case 2: Argentina above Spain

The model has Argentina 1st (3.15) over Spain (2.98), while the outright winner market ranks them the other way. Argentina's edge is defensive (0.38 goals conceded per match against Spain's 0.82), but that record is built 77% against CONMEBOL and CONCACAF opposition, and comparing it with Spain's depends on the very few matches that pit the two confederations against each other. The market only corrects the group stage, where it tilts the balance toward Spain; the knockout pairings carry no odds, and that is where Argentina overtakes. That is why its champion probability barely changes with or without the market (16.6%; Spain 13.6%).

How to read it

Rankings within each bloc are reliable — intra-UEFA or intra-CONMEBOL density makes the opponent discount work (England's soft schedule, mean opponent 1.55 vs Spain's 1.86, is correctly discounted within UEFA). What is fragile is the offset between scales: the 16.6% vs 13.6% for Argentina-Spain should be read as a statistical tie with a nominal edge, and the market ordering is at least as credible. The same applies to the high ratings of Morocco (8th, mean opponent 1.05 — the weakest schedule in the top ranks) and Japan (12th, 1.19).

§7The robustness study and why it was closed

A systematic four-phase study was run against the anchoring problem (docs/model-robustness-plan.md), with a single validation check — the rolling backtest, measuring RPS and log-loss plus a purpose-built cross-confederation bias audit — and a strict reproducibility rule: every intervention sits behind a switch whose default reproduces the current model exactly.

Study conclusion

No anchor — internal or external — among those tested corrects the CONMEBOL/CONCACAF-vs-UEFA bias without degrading calibration: the dataset's own bridges are too thin, and the natural external reference (Elo) suffers from the same bias. The limitation is accepted and documented, mitigated in practice by the market blend (ODDS_WEIGHT = 1.0) wherever odds exist.

§8Other limitations

  • Knockouts at a neutral venue. Host advantage is modelled only in the groups: the knockout bracket is synthetic ("winner of group E", "best third assigned to match 80") with no venue attached, so every tie is played neutral. Net effect: a small, systematic under-rating of the deep runs of the USA, Mexico and Canada.
  • No odds on potential pairings. Odds feeds cover only scheduled matches, so from the Round of 32 onward the simulation is model-only even with odds in the group stage — and therefore inherits the bias of section 6 in full (the mechanism of the Argentina-Spain case).
  • xG with no track record (section 5): partial coverage (from ~mid-2022, no friendlies) and a net cost in points. Off for 2026.
